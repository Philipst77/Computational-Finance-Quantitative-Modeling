# Black-Scholes Option Pricing

The Black-Scholes model provides a closed-form solution for pricing European options by assuming that stock prices follow geometric Brownian motion with constant volatility. Under the risk-neutral pricing framework, the model calculates the fair value of a call option by taking the difference between two components: the expected present value of receiving the stock conditional on exercise, and the expected present value of paying the strike price. These expectations are computed using the cumulative standard normal distribution evaluated at specific values that depend on the current stock price, strike price, risk-free rate, volatility, and time to maturity. The formula transforms the complex problem of valuing contingent claims into a tractable mathematical expression that can be computed instantly.

The power of Black-Scholes lies in its ability to decompose option pricing into measurable sensitivities known as the Greeks. Delta measures how the option price changes with the stock price, Gamma captures the curvature or how Delta itself changes, Vega quantifies sensitivity to volatility changes, and Theta represents time decay. Near the strike price, all these sensitivities are at their maximum, making at-the-money options the most responsive to market movements. The model's surface visualization reveals how option values smoothly vary across different stock prices and volatility levels, with higher volatility increasing option values due to the asymmetric payoff structure where upside is unlimited but downside is capped at the premium paid. This framework remains foundational in quantitative finance, providing both theoretical insight and practical tools for pricing, hedging, and risk management.
