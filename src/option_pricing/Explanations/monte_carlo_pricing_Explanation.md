# Monte Carlo Option Pricing

Monte Carlo simulation is used to price options by generating thousands of random future stock price paths and averaging their discounted payoffs. Unlike closed-form solutions, Monte Carlo is flexible and can handle path-dependent and exotic options.

Under risk-neutral pricing, a European call option's value equals the expected discounted payoff under the risk-neutral measure. The terminal stock price follows a log-normal distribution derived from geometric Brownian motion, where the drift equals the risk-free rate. This drift adjustment ensures the discounted stock price is a martingale, maintaining arbitrage-free pricing. The randomness comes from a standard normal variable scaled by volatility and the square root of time.

The Monte Carlo algorithm works by first generating many independent standard normal random variables, each representing a different possible market scenario. For each scenario, the terminal stock price is calculated by exponentiating the drift-adjusted return plus the random shock. The option payoff is then computed as the maximum of the stock price minus strike price and zero, since call options are never exercised at a loss. These payoffs are discounted back to present value using the risk-free rate, and finally averaged across all scenarios. By the law of large numbers, as the number of simulations increases, the sample mean converges to the true option price with error decreasing proportionally to one over the square root of the number of paths.

The Monte Carlo estimator is unbiased, meaning its expected value equals the true option price. Its standard error is given by the standard deviation of the discounted payoffs divided by the square root of the number of simulations. This standard error quantifies estimation uncertainty and allows construction of confidence intervals. With one hundred thousand paths, the standard error is typically a few cents for typical option parameters, providing accurate pricing suitable for trading and risk management applications.
