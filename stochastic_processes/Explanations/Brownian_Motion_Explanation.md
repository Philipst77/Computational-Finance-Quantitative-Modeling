# Brownian Motion and Its Role in Financial Modeling

We simulate Brownian motion first because it is the fundamental mathematical model of accumulated randomness over time. It represents the sum of many small, independent random shocks, whose increments are normally distributed. This makes Brownian motion the natural building block for financial models, since uncertainty in markets arises from the accumulation of many independent and unpredictable events.

When simulating Brownian motion numerically, time is divided into small intervals of length **dt**. At each step, randomness is introduced using a standard normal variable scaled by **√dt**. This scaling is essential: a standard normal random variable has variance 1, while multiplying it by **√dt** produces variance **dt**. As a result, each time step contributes the correct amount of uncertainty, and when many steps are summed together, the total variance grows linearly with time, matching the defining property of Brownian motion that the variance over an interval of length **T** equals **T**.

Brownian motion is not a collection of independent values, but a path. Each step represents the change in randomness since the previous moment, and the process is constructed by taking a cumulative sum of these increments. This accumulation is what gives Brownian motion memory in time: the current value reflects the sum of all past random shocks. Without the cumulative sum, there would be no evolving trajectory, only isolated random samples.

Because each time step adds a new independent shock with positive variance, uncertainty grows as time progresses. Short-term outcomes are relatively concentrated, while long-term outcomes become increasingly spread out. This property is fundamental in finance, as it captures the intuition that the further into the future one looks, the less certain asset prices become.

The resulting paths appear continuous but highly irregular. As the time step **dt** becomes smaller, individual changes become infinitesimal, so the path has no jumps and never "teleports." At the same time, randomness exists at every scale, so the path is nowhere smooth: zooming in reveals the same jagged behavior seen at larger scales. Brownian motion has no well-defined slope and exhibits infinite variation, a property that closely mirrors the constant, twitchy fluctuations observed in real financial markets.

In finance, prices evolve multiplicatively rather than additively, so models are formulated in log-price space, where multiplication becomes addition. Geometric Brownian Motion builds directly on Brownian motion by combining drift, volatility, and accumulated randomness to model realistic stock price behavior. In this framework, Brownian motion captures the unpredictable shocks to log-returns, while drift and volatility control the average growth and dispersion of prices.

Brownian motion is chaotic at every small time step, which is why individual movements are random and unpredictable. There is no smooth direction or trend locally. When viewed over longer time scales, the path may appear smooth, but it is never actually smooth — randomness exists at every level. This behavior closely mirrors real financial markets, where prices fluctuate unpredictably in the short term but exhibit statistical structure over time. That is why Brownian motion is used as the foundation for stock price simulation models.
